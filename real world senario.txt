The real-world scenario
Imagine you're a student at ENS Marrakech. You want to know where is the campus shuttle right now and when will it arrive at the library stop. That's the whole project.
There are 3 people involved:

You (student) — watching the shuttle on your phone
The driver — driving and sharing his GPS location
The admin — managing everything from a web page


What each table represents
shuttles — the physical bus itself
Think of it as the vehicle registration card. It stores information about the bus that exists in real life — its name, plate number, how many seats it has, and who drives it. This doesn't change often.
Navette 1 | A-1234-B | 25 seats | Driver: Mohammed
Navette 2 | B-5678-C | 25 seats | Driver: Youssef

stops — the bus stops around campus
Like a map of all the stops. Each stop has a name and GPS coordinates so it can appear on the map. They also have an order — stop 1 is the first, stop 2 is second, etc.
1. Entrée principale  → lat: 31.6295, lng: -7.9811
2. Bibliothèque       → lat: 31.6300, lng: -7.9805
3. Restaurant         → lat: 31.6290, lng: -7.9820

shuttle_trips — one specific journey on one specific day
This is the most important table to understand. A shuttle doesn't just exist — it goes on trips. Every time a driver starts his shift and begins driving, that creates a new trip. Think of it like a taxi meter being turned on.
Trip #1 → Navette 1 | started 08:00 | status: active
Trip #2 → Navette 1 | started 14:00 | status: finished
Trip #3 → Navette 2 | started 09:00 | status: paused
The same shuttle (Navette 1) can have many trips over many days — one trip per shift. When the driver clicks "start" on his phone, a new row is created here. When he clicks "finished", ended_at gets filled in.

positions — the GPS breadcrumbs
Every 5–10 seconds while the driver is moving, his phone sends its GPS location. Each one is saved here as a row. This is what allows students to see the shuttle moving in real time on the map.
Trip #1 | lat: 31.6295 | lng: -7.9811 | 08:00:05
Trip #1 | lat: 31.6296 | lng: -7.9812 | 08:00:10
Trip #1 | lat: 31.6297 | lng: -7.9813 | 08:00:15
This table grows very fast — thousands of rows per day. That's normal.

How they connect
Here's the flow from the moment the driver starts to the moment you see the shuttle on your map:
Driver starts shift
        ↓
New row in shuttle_trips  ← linked to shuttles (which bus)
        ↓
Every 5 seconds, phone sends GPS
        ↓
New row in positions  ← linked to shuttle_trips (which trip)
        ↓
Your app asks: "where is Navette 1 right now?"
        ↓
API finds the LATEST position row for the active trip of Navette 1
        ↓
Your map shows the bus icon at those coordinates
